About

Career shape

Breadth with a pattern.

I started in New York in beauty and cosmetics, working through executive support, marketing analysis, product development coordination, product launches, promotional analysis, and retail execution. I learned early that commercial performance is a chain: product, packaging, timing, retailer execution, inventory, replenishment, sell-through, reporting, and follow-up.

Later, in wine, beverage, and hospitality, I worked closer to the customer. Sales, CRM, wine clubs, compliance, service, and high-pressure hospitality taught me that execution is physical before it is digital. Someone has to make the thing work in front of the customer.

At L'Oreal / SalonCentric, I moved deep into commercial finance and trade governance. I governed promotional finance and trade execution for a $394M business unit inside a $1.1B channel operation. The work involved AP, AR, manufacturer agreements, distribution centers, customer settlement, SAP, Vistex, claims, deductions, early payment discounts, accruals, Gross-to-Net reporting, and operational mess that never appears in a clean process diagram.

After moving to France, I worked in education and research, teaching finance, FP&A, marketing, consumer behavior, digital systems, and scenario-based decision-making. Teaching forced me to make complex systems explainable.

In RGM and TPM/TPO platform environments, I worked across multi-market CPG deployments where commercial logic, ERP behavior, master data, and user adoption all had to align. That is where many transformation efforts break down: the platform is live, the process exists, the training happened, and yet the business still does not trust the result.

How I think

The operating beliefs.

The system is always bigger than the software.

Most problems blamed on platforms are actually problems of commercial logic, governance, incentives, or data readiness.

Commercial decisions have financial consequences.

If the pricing logic, funding rule, promotional mechanic, or settlement path is unclear, the P&L will eventually tell the truth.

Workarounds are evidence.

A workaround tells you where the process, system, data, or incentive structure failed to match reality.

Teaching sharpens execution.

If you cannot explain the system clearly, you probably do not understand it well enough to govern it.

AI is a tool, not a priesthood.

AI is useful when it improves reasoning, visibility, and decision quality. It is dangerous when it adds polish to weak thinking.

Operating style

Direct, practical, and allergic to theater. I ask what must be true, what will break, who owns the decision, what the system will do, and where the financial consequence lands.
