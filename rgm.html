RGM / TPM Practitioner Briefing

RGM is not a dashboard. TPM is not a workflow.

These systems only work when commercial logic, data readiness, ERP integration, decision rights, user incentives, and financial controls align.

Most failed programs do not fail because the software cannot do anything useful.

They fail because the business has not made the underlying operating logic clear enough for the system to execute.

Objective

What RGM / TPM is trying to accomplish.

A good RGM / TPM environment bridges what was negotiated with a customer and what actually gets executed in the market.

It must capture promotion mechanics, funding allocations, performance expectations, approval rules, customer structures, product structures, pricing logic, accrual behavior, settlement paths, and financial outcomes.

Commercial Logic -> Execution -> Accruals / Settlement -> Revenue Plan -> P&L / Balance Sheet.

The chain looks simple. Every arrow is a failure point.

What breaks

The recurring failure points.

Commercial Logic Is Unresolved

The business cannot clearly explain how promotions are planned, approved, funded, executed, settled, and measured.

Data Is Assumed Ready

Customer hierarchies, product hierarchies, pricing conditions, and historical baselines are treated as reliable until UAT proves otherwise.

ERP Is Treated as Downstream

ERP integration is treated as a technical detail instead of a design input. That is how design choices become financial defects.

Stakeholders Are Politely Misaligned

Sales, Finance, IT, and leadership appear aligned until a real trade-off is required.

Success Is Undefined

Go-live happens, but no one agrees whether the project actually worked.

Pre-design readiness

Before design begins, six domains need clarity.

01 - Commercial Operating Model

How are promotions planned, approved, executed, and settled today? Who owns decisions? What must change?

02 - Data Readiness

Are customer hierarchies, product hierarchies, pricing conditions, historical baselines, and sell-in / sell-through / sell-out data usable?

03 - ERP Integration Landscape

Which ERP modules are in scope? What must the TPM-to-ERP flow support? Who owns the integration layer?

04 - Stakeholder Alignment

Are Sales, Finance, IT, and leadership aligned on goals, trade-offs, definitions, and what success actually means?

05 - Current-State Reality

What does the business actually do today, not what it says it does in a workshop?

06 - Definition of Done

How will value be measured? What is the baseline? Who signs off that the outcome was achieved?

Advisor role

The commercial logic guardian.

The advisor role is not project management, configuration coordination, or vendor apologetics. The advisor ensures the design reflects how the client's business actually operates, not how a platform template assumes it operates.

  • Challenge whether the as-is process is real or aspirational.
  • Check whether proposed design will work downstream in ERP.
  • Validate whether financial outcomes will be accurate and auditable.
  • Confirm UAT scenarios cover real commercial edge cases.

One question matters: Does this design reflect the commercial intent of the business, or the default behavior of the platform?

UAT and Defect Validation

UAT is where bad assumptions send their invoices. A strong process tests commercial edge cases, financial postings, accrual behavior, settlement paths, claims behavior, master data dependencies, and user decisions under realistic pressure.

Not every issue is a bug. It may be data, design, configuration, change request, process misunderstanding, training, adoption, or unresolved commercial logic.

Change Management

Change management begins at Day 0. It is not communications. It is governance.

A TPM implementation changes how people plan, approve, fund, execute, settle, measure, and explain commercial activity. Go-live is not the end of the change. It is the beginning of the hardest part.
